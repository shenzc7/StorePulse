NB-INGARCH Model

Literature Review, Comparative Analysis & Benchmarks

Model Description & Theoretical Framework

The NB-INGARCH (Negative Binomial Integer-valued GARCH) model represents a sophisticated statistical approach for modeling discrete time series data. Unlike standard ARIMA or GARCH models that assume continuous data, the NB-INGARCH framework is explicitly designed for count data—non-negative integers representing events like customer footfall.

Core Mechanics: The model operates on the principle that the current expected count ($\lambda_t$) depends on both past observations (Autoregressive component) and past expected counts (GARCH component). This allows the model to "remember" periods of high activity and adjust its baseline expectations accordingly.

Handling Overdispersion: In retail analytics, data is rarely "well-behaved." It exhibits overdispersion, where the variance exceeds the mean ($Var(Y_t) > E[Y_t]$). A standard Poisson model assumes equidispersion ($Var = Mean$), making it brittle for real-world data. The Negative Binomial distribution introduces a dispersion parameter ($\phi$), allowing the model to flexibly accommodate the "bursty" nature of store traffic—capturing sudden spikes (e.g., promotions) and deep lulls without breaking statistical assumptions.

Technical Justification

Equation Form: λt = ω + ∑ αi Yt-i + ∑ βj λt-j

By utilizing the Negative Binomial distribution Yt ~ NB(λt, φ), we decouple the mean from the variance. This provides a statistically rigorous foundation for forecasting in volatile environments, ensuring that confidence intervals are wide enough to capture risk during peak periods but tight enough to be useful during stable periods.

Model Advantages

Key Strengths

  • Handles Overdispersion: Explicitly models variance > mean, preventing underestimation of volatility.
  • Integer-Valued: Respects the discrete nature of count data (cannot have 2.5 customers).
  • Volatility Clustering: Captures time-dependent variance (e.g., busy weekends followed by quiet Mondays).
  • Robustness: Less sensitive to outliers compared to standard Poisson models.
